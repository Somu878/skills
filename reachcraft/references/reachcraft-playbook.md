# ReachCraft Playbook

Use this when drafting, rewriting, or planning X content. Default to reach-first packaging: make the post understandable, inspectable, reply-worthy, and follow-worthy from the available context.

## Reach-First Principle

The job is not only to summarize context. The job is to turn context into a post likely to get more impressions from the right audience.

Use the context as raw material, then package it with:

- a first line that stops the target reader
- clear topic keywords for semantic retrieval
- a concrete problem, result, artifact, or opinion
- a reason to reply, repost, click, inspect media, visit the profile, or follow
- enough proof to avoid sounding like generic AI content

If the context is weak, improve the packaging. If the context is unclear, infer a reasonable angle and state the assumption briefly.

## Engagement Model

Judge every draft against these questions:

- Dwell: Would the first line make the right person stop?
- Reply: Is there a specific tension, question, claim, or open loop worth responding to?
- Repost/share: Is the idea useful, status-enhancing, surprising, funny, or identity-relevant?
- Click/profile click: Does the post make the author seem worth checking out?
- Follow: Does it reinforce a repeatable account promise?
- Negative feedback: Could it feel misleading, generic, spammy, hostile, or low signal?

## Impression Checklist

Before finalizing, check:

- Audience: Is it obvious who should care?
- Hook: Does line one create pain, curiosity, contrast, or proof?
- Keywords: Does it naturally include the topic words people and models use?
- Specificity: Is there at least one concrete noun, metric, feature, artifact, or example?
- Action path: Is there a reason to reply, repost, click, inspect media, or follow?
- Freshness: Does it feel timely, newly built, newly learned, or practically useful?
- Negative risk: Would the right audience feel baited, misled, or bored?

If two or more fail, rewrite before answering.

## Idea-To-Post Workflow

1. Name the core idea in one sentence.
2. Pick the audience: who should care now?
3. Choose the reach angle:
   - Pain-first
   - Outcome-first
   - Proof-first
   - Builder-log
   - Contrarian but fair
   - Tutorial/useful
   - Question/community
4. Choose the content angle:
   - Build in public
   - Lesson learned
   - Sharp opinion
   - Mistake or failure
   - Before/after
   - Mini tutorial
   - Launch/update
   - Contrarian but fair take
   - Personal story with takeaway
5. Add proof: screenshot, metric, demo, repo, code snippet, customer quote, failure, or concrete example.
6. Write the post with one clear job: teach, provoke, announce, ask, or recruit.
7. Add an engagement path: question, useful resource, reply prompt, first reply, or follow-up promise.

## Strong Single-Post Patterns

### Build Log

`I built [thing] because [specific pain].`

`The weird part: [surprising constraint/result].`

`Here is what changed: [proof/result].`

### Lesson

`I thought [old belief].`

`Then [specific experience] changed my mind.`

`Now I do [new behavior].`

### Sharp POV

`Most people are wrong about [topic].`

`The problem is not [common explanation].`

`It is [specific mechanism].`

### Mini Tutorial

`If you are building [thing], do this before [common step]:`

`1. [step]`
`2. [step]`
`3. [step]`

`It saves [specific cost/time/risk].`

### Question That Earns Replies

Ask questions with constraints. Avoid generic "thoughts?"

Better:

- `What is the one AI tool you still refuse to replace with an agent?`
- `If you had 7 days to launch an AI micro-SaaS, what would you build first?`
- `Solana builders: what is still weirdly painful in onboarding?`

## Thread Pattern

Use a thread when the idea needs sequence or proof.

1. Hook: specific promise or tension.
2. Context: why this matters now.
3. Lesson 1: concrete, short.
4. Lesson 2: concrete, short.
5. Example/proof: screenshot, result, or story.
6. Takeaway: one memorable line.
7. CTA: ask a real question, invite feedback, or offer the resource.

Keep each post in the thread focused on one point.

## Rewrite Checklist

When improving a post:

- Move the strongest claim to the first line.
- Replace generic nouns with concrete nouns.
- Remove throat-clearing like "I just wanted to share".
- Cut filler adjectives such as amazing, game-changing, revolutionary, insane, unless backed by proof.
- Add one of: number, timeline, result, mistake, contrast, artifact, or named audience.
- Make the ask natural and specific.
- Preserve the user's voice unless they request a new style.

## Hook Bank

- `The problem with [category] is not [obvious thing]. It is [real bottleneck].`
- `[Audience] keep running into this: [pain].`
- `I built [thing] because [specific pain].`
- `[Thing] sounds simple until [hard edge case].`
- `Most [category] demos miss [specific reality].`
- `I changed my mind about [topic].`
- `[Tool/category] is not the hard part. [Specific bottleneck] is.`
- `The fastest way to learn [skill] is not [common advice]. It is [specific action].`
- `I spent [time] building [thing]. The surprising lesson: [lesson].`
- `Tiny product idea: [idea].`
- `A mistake I keep seeing AI builders make: [mistake].`
- `If I were starting [thing] from zero today, I would do this:`
- `[N] things I wish I knew before building [thing]:`
- `Hot take: [fair contrarian opinion].`
- `This is either obvious or useful, but [insight].`

## Reply Strategy

Use replies to enter relevant graphs:

- Reply to bigger accounts in AI, indie hacking, product, Solana, and builder communities.
- Prioritize fresh posts with active replies.
- Add one concrete thing: example, counterexample, useful link, screenshot, or question.
- Do not drop links unless the link is requested or clearly useful.
- Turn high-performing replies into standalone posts.

For the user's own post, add a first reply when the main post would get cluttered. Use it for:

- feature list
- link
- technical details
- training/eval notes
- "how it works"
- a constrained question
- screenshot or diagram context

Reply styles:

- Additive: `This matches what I saw with [specific example].`
- Respectful disagreement: `I agree with [part], but I think [part] is underrated because [reason].`
- Builder proof: `I tried this last week. The bottleneck was [specific].`
- Clarifying question: `When you say [term], do you mean [A] or [B]?`

## Weekly Posting System

For early-stage builder accounts:

- 2 build logs
- 2 lessons or mini tutorials
- 1 strong opinion
- 1 question/post designed for replies
- 1 personal/proof post
- 10-20 thoughtful replies per day to relevant accounts

Repeat winners. Change packaging, not the core niche.

## Media Guidance

Suggest media when it strengthens proof or comprehension:

- Product screenshot for launches and build logs.
- Short demo video for tools and workflows.
- Diagram for systems, algorithms, or mental models.
- Before/after image for improvements.
- Code snippet only when the target audience is technical.

Avoid decorative images that do not make the claim more credible.

## Link Posts

Links can reduce visible engagement if the post feels like a link dump. When sharing a model, repo, blog, or product:

1. Put the human problem in the first line.
2. Explain the artifact in plain English.
3. Mention how it was built only after the problem is clear.
4. Put the link at the end, or in the first reply if the user wants a cleaner main post.
5. Use the first reply for details that help builders decide whether to click.

Pattern:

`[Audience] still struggle with [pain].`

`I built [artifact]: [plain-English function].`

`It uses [tech/proof] to [outcome].`

`[link or first-reply CTA]`

## Guardrails

Do not optimize for cheap engagement that attracts negative feedback. Avoid:

- fake scarcity
- engagement bait
- copied viral formats without substance
- outrage without an earned point
- unrelated trend-jacking
- mass generic replies
- misleading metrics
- AI-slop phrasing
- claims that cannot be defended

Prefer durable audience trust over one noisy post.
